How to Write a Content Brief in Under 5 Minutes With AI

A content brief is the planning document that separates posts that rank from posts that don't. The problem has always been time — building a proper brief used to take 30 to 60 minutes of research and formatting.

With AI, that time drops to under five minutes. Here is exactly how.

What You Need Before You Start

Just one thing: your target keyword. Everything else — the structure, the intent analysis, the subheadings, the angle — the AI generates from that single input.

The 5-Minute Process

Open ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Any of the three will work. Free versions are sufficient for this task.
Paste the prompt below with your keyword filled in.
Review the output. Read through the structure and adjust any section that does not fit your angle or audience.
Copy the brief into your writing document. Use the subheadings as your post skeleton and start writing section by section.
Write from the brief, not around it. Every section has a defined purpose — stick to it.

The Prompt

Create a content brief for a blog post targeting the keyword "{your keyword}".

Include:
- Primary search intent
- H1 title (keyword included naturally)
- H2 subheadings (5 to 6 sections)
- Under each H2: 2 bullet points of what to cover
- 3 questions the post must answer to satisfy the reader
- One content angle that differentiates this post
- Suggested word count based on what ranks for this keyword

What the Output Gives You

A complete writing blueprint. The H2 subheadings become your document structure. The bullet points tell you what each section must cover. The three questions keep you focused on the reader. The differentiating angle keeps your post from being a copy of everything else.

You go from keyword to writing-ready in under five minutes. The writing itself becomes faster too — because you are filling in a structure, not inventing one as you go.

One Thing to Adjust

AI-generated subheadings can sometimes be generic. If a suggested H2 feels like it could belong to any post on any blog, rewrite it to be more specific to your angle and audience. The brief is a starting point — your editorial judgment shapes the final structure.
